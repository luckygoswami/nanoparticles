General view of possible interactions, routes of exposure, and adverse outcomes that can be triggered by exposure of humans and the environment to nanomaterials.

Nanosafety is a multidisciplinary field that focuses on assessing and managing the potential risks associated with nanotechnology, particularly the use of nanoparticles and nanomaterials. As nanotechnology continues to advance and find applications in various industries such as healthcare, electronics, and materials science, understanding and addressing safety concerns become crucial.

Nanoparticles

are particles with dimensions typically ranging from 1 to 100 nanometers (nm) in at least one dimension. They exhibit unique physical and chemical properties due to their small size and large surface area-to-volume ratio, making them desirable for various applications but also raising concerns about potential health and environmental impacts.

Nanomaterials

refer to materials that contain nanoparticles or structures with dimensions at the nanoscale. They can be engineered to have specific properties for use in electronics, medicine, cosmetics, and other fields. Common types of nanomaterials include carbon nanotubes, quantum dots, and metal nanoparticles.

Nanotoxicology

is the study of the toxicity and biological effects of nanomaterials on living organisms, including humans and the environment. It involves investigating how nanoparticles interact with cells, tissues, and biological systems, as well as understanding the mechanisms of toxicity and potential health risks associated with exposure.
